Mask Redistribution Simulator

SJTU VG101 Intro to Programming Course Project (2020)

My Role: Modeling, Visualization

Overview

I started learning computer programming during the devastating COVID-19 outbreak. My teammates and I were deeply shocked to learn about the heartbreaking death rate in Hubei Province caused by sudden infection spikes and mask shortages. This inspired us to create a project in C++ with OpenGL to simulate how varying production capacities, transportation routes, and population behaviors might affect the spread of the virus. Our goal was to predict a more efficient mask distribution network for Hubei Province, China.

Modeling

We used the classical Susceptible–Infected–Recovered (SIR) Model to simulate the dynamics of COVID-19 infection. Defining the population of susceptible, infected, and recovered people to be $S$, $I$, and $R$ respectively (total population $N$), their interactions can be described with the following equations: $$ \frac{dS}{dt} = -\frac{\beta SI}{N}, \quad \frac{dI}{dt} = \frac{\beta SI}{N} - \gamma I, \quad \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I $$ Here, $\beta$ represents the effective contact rate, which is adjusted by mask availability, and $\gamma$ represents the mean recovery rate, which would depend on hospital capacity and evolves with time based on our assumptions.

Three main factors influence mask distribution modeling:

  • Consumption: A daily mask usage rate based on whether individuals are susceptible, infected, or recovered.
  • Production: Major industrial cities produce masks daily, adding to their inventories over time.
  • Transportation (To moderate):
    • Between Major and Non-Major Cities (red roads): Masks are prioritized for cities with the most urgent infection growth rates $\tfrac{dI}{dt}$. Surplus masks are sent to less-urgent cities if stock remains.
    • Within Regional Networks (yellow roads): Smaller-scale routes supply neighboring cities every two hours.

These factors collectively adjust each city’s mask inventory, affecting $\beta$ in the SIR model and influencing each city’s infection trajectory.

User Interface

  • Initial Input Screen: Users specify city types (major industrial city vs. regular cities), mask production capacities, and a tolerable infection ratio. They can click on fields to enter values and press “OK” to start the simulation.
  • Main Simulation Dashboard:
    • Message Box: Displays updates on simulation progress and outcome reasons (e.g., a city surpassing the infection threshold, or no more active infections).
    • Map of Hubei:
      • Color-coded to represent infection severity in each city (darker shades = higher infection).
      • Two types of roads appear, each with its own animation and usage rules (red for large-scale mask shipments from Xiantao every 6 hours, yellow for smaller shipments within regional networks every 2 hours).
      • The simulation runs over time steps, updating the map’s color gradients, animating road usage, and refreshing data metrics to mirror the evolving infection and resource distribution landscape.
    • Data List: Shows four critical metrics (total population, number of infected, number of recovered, mask inventory) for each city.
    • Interactive Features: An “i” (information) button provides guidance; clicking on a city offers infection trends for that specific city.
