Personified Goal Tracking Experience with Decreasing Obtrusiveness

CMU 05-685 Prototyping Algorithmic Experiences Individual Project (2024)

Overview

Many people struggle to achieve personal goals, whether it’s eating healthier, exercising consistently, or staying organized with daily tasks. This project introduces a prototype for an active and immersive goal-tracking experience, featuring a goal tracker personified as celebrity avatars the user may feel connected to. Inspired by the concept of fading, the tracker gradually becomes less obstrusive as the user establishes consistent habits. By implementing this in VR, I explored whether extended, immersive interactions with celebrity avatars could enhance user motivation and engagement.

Prototyping Study

Three VR scenes were created and tested:

  • Main Scene
    • Users engage in routine activities (e.g., watching YouTube).
    • An always-on goal tracker (timer + daily goals) stays in their peripheral vision.
    • When it’s time to work on a goal, the relevant celebrity avatar appears to prompt the user. The voice was generated via Google Text-to-Speech. Users can either follow the prompt or ignore it.
  • Lunch Scene (ft. Gordon Ramsay)
    • Users choose from different foods (e.g., soda, bananas, burgers).
    • Gordon Ramsay’s voice line changes based on whether the user picks healthy or unhealthy items.
  • Exercise Scene (ft. LeBron James)
    • Users practice shooting basketballs while LeBron James provides ongoing encouragement or urges them to continue if they pause.

The implementation was based on Unity and Oculus Quest 2. During the study session, I faked the AI (in a Wizard-of-Oz fashion) by monitoring each participant’s actions (via a live mirror of the VR view) and deciding when they had formed a habit—thus triggering the AI fade-out. If participants succeeded in completing tasks, the avatar would disappear on the next “day.”

Insights

  • Celebrity Avatars: While some participants enjoyed or tolerated Gordon and LeBron’s presence, others found them unsettling or unmotivating—particularly when the avatars repeatedly prompted them. Some participants did not feel a strong connection to these avatars, attributing to the potential uncanny valley effect.
  • Habit Formation & Timing: Using just one simulated day as the threshold to fade the AI felt too brief to establish genuine habits. Participants suggested longer timelines or more nuanced criteria.
  • Obtrusive Interface Design: The large, in-view goal-tracking interface blocked participants’ vision. They suggested a more aesthetic, less distracting design to avoid feeling overwhelmed.
  • Realism of VR (Meta-level Reflection): Despite VR’s immersive potential, some participants did not feel the scenarios were realistic enough to simulate day-to-day activity. They also felt the tasks functioned more like short “demos” than a genuine reflection of real life.
